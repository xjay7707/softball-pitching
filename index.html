Read This First

WELCOME, PITCHER 🚀

You're about to learn one of the hardest and most important positions in softball. Take it slow, trust the process, and you'll build a foundation that lasts your whole career.

What Makes Pitching Different

Pitching in fastpitch softball uses an underhand windmill motion — your arm makes a full circle and you release the ball down by your hip. It looks simple when someone's good at it. It is not simple. It's a full-body athletic movement that takes time to build.

Here's the good news: you already have a huge head start. You've played this game at a high level. You understand the field, you have body control, and you've got a strong arm from catching and playing the corners. That athleticism is exactly what great pitchers are built from. Now we're going to teach your body a new skill on top of it.

This guide takes you from day one all the way to throwing a fastball you can locate and a change-up you can trust. Go in order. Don't skip the boring stuff. The pitchers who rush the fundamentals are the ones who develop bad habits that take years to fix.

🛡️ Arm Safety — Non-Negotiable

  • Never pitch through sharp or joint pain. Sore muscles are normal, joint pain is not.
  • Always warm up your arm BEFORE pitching. Never cold-throw at full speed.
  • Build volume slowly. More pitches too soon is the #1 cause of young arm injuries.
  • Take rest days seriously. Your arm gets stronger when it recovers, not when it's overworked.
  • Ice your shoulder or forearm if it's sore after a heavy session.

How To Use This Guide

If you're brand new: Start with Arm Care, then Mechanics. Spend your first two to three weeks ONLY on the windmill motion and wrist snaps before you worry about speed or spots. The Planner tab lays this out for you week by week.

A big focus of this guide is what you can do on your own. A lot of pitching practice does need a catcher or a net, but plenty doesn't. Every drill in this guide is labeled — look for the 🙋 Solo tag for the things you can do completely alone, even in your room or driveway.

EVERY GREAT PITCHER STARTED AT ZERO 🔥

The girl throwing 60 mph in high school was once exactly where you are right now — learning the circle, snapping her wrist, figuring it out one rep at a time. The difference between her and everyone else was that she kept showing up. That's the whole secret. Let's get to work.

Step 1 · Every Session

ARM CARE & WARM-UP 🔥

Your arm is your engine. Pitchers who take care of their arms have long careers. Pitchers who skip this get hurt. This routine comes BEFORE every single throwing session, no exceptions.

Pitching is full-body. Wake up everything before you touch your arm.

🧊

AFTER YOU PITCH — RECOVERY

⭐ Don't Skip
⚡ Why

What you do after pitching matters as much as the warm-up. Good recovery habits now build an arm that stays healthy for years.

  • 1
    Light band work again — same external rotations, slow and easy. This flushes the arm out.
  • 2
    Gentle stretching — shoulder, forearm, wrist. Hold each 20–30 seconds.
  • 3
    Ice if sore — 15 minutes on the shoulder or forearm if you had a heavy session or feel achy.
  • 4
    Hydrate and rest. Muscles rebuild with water and sleep. This is when you actually get stronger.
HEALTHY ARM = LONG CAREER 💪

The best ability is availability. A pitcher who can't throw because her arm hurts can't help her team. Treat your arm like the valuable tool it is, every single day.

The Foundation · Master This First

WINDMILL MECHANICS ⚙️

The windmill pitch breaks down into 6 phases. Learn each one slowly and in order. Speed comes LATER — clean mechanics come first. Rushing this is the #1 mistake new pitchers make.

Phase 1

🧍 STANCE & SET

  • Both feet on the pitching rubber, shoulder-width
  • Throwing-side foot forward, toes on top of rubber
  • Back foot toe touching the back edge
  • Shoulders square to the plate (catcher)
  • Weight balanced, relaxed, athletic
  • Ball in glove, both hands at your belt
Phase 2

🤝 PRESENTATION

  • Present the ball — hands together out front briefly
  • This is required by the rules (a pause)
  • Take a breath, find your target
  • Small weight rock to gather momentum
  • Stay relaxed — tension kills speed
  • Eyes locked on your spot
⭐ Phase 3 — POWER SOURCE

👟 STRIDE & DRIVE

  • Explode forward off the rubber with your back leg
  • Drive your stride foot OUT toward the plate
  • Longer stride = more power (aim to stretch it)
  • Stride foot lands slightly open (10–20°)
  • This leg drive is where REAL speed comes from
  • Hands separate as you start the circle
Phase 4

🌀 ARM CIRCLE

  • Arm makes a full, fast circle up and over
  • Keep arm close to your head/ear at the top
  • Stay LOOSE — "loose arm = fast arm"
  • Don't muscle it — let it whip naturally
  • Arm stays straight but relaxed, not stiff
  • Glove side pulls in to your hip (drives rotation)
⭐ Phase 5 — THE MONEY

💥 RELEASE & SNAP

  • Ball releases right at your hip, out in front
  • Forearm "brushes" past your hip on the way through
  • SNAP your wrist hard at release — this is huge
  • Wrist snap creates both speed AND spin
  • Think "snap the door shut" with your wrist
  • Release point in front = strikes, behind = high
Phase 6

🎯 FOLLOW-THROUGH

  • Arm continues up naturally after release
  • Weight fully transfers onto stride leg
  • Back foot drags off the rubber and comes through
  • Square up and get into fielding position FAST
  • You're a fielder the moment you release
  • Balanced finish, ready for a comebacker
SLOW IS SMOOTH. SMOOTH IS FAST. 🎯

Build each phase at half speed until it feels natural, THEN add speed. A pitcher with clean mechanics at 40 mph will throw 55 mph in a year. A pitcher with sloppy mechanics at 45 mph will be stuck forever. Build it right.

Pitch #1 · Your Foundation

THE FASTBALL 🎯

Every pitcher's foundation. Before any movement pitch, you master the fastball — the grip, locating it where you want, and building velocity. This is 80% of your job as a young pitcher.

🤚

THE 4-SEAM GRIP

⭐ Start Here
⚡ Why

The 4-seam grip gives you the most backspin and the truest, fastest, straightest ball. It's the most accurate grip, which is exactly what a young pitcher needs while building consistency.

  • 1
    Find the "horseshoe" or "C" shape in the seams. Lay your fingertips across the seams where they're widest.
  • 2
    Place your first two or three fingers across the seam, fingertips ON the threads. Your fingertips grip and snap the seam.
  • 3
    Thumb underneath for support, resting on a seam or smooth leather. Comfortable, not squeezing.
  • 4
    Grip with your fingers, not your palm. There should be a small gap between the ball and your palm. This lets you snap.
  • 5
    Hold it firm but relaxed. A death grip slows your wrist snap. Think "firm handshake," not "crushing a can."
⚡

HOW TO THROW HARDER

⭐ Velocity Builders
⚡ Why

Speed is NOT about throwing your arm harder. It comes from your whole body working together. Here's where real velocity actually comes from, in order of importance.

  • 1
    Leg drive. Your legs are your engine. Explode off the rubber. The harder you drive forward, the more power transfers to the ball.
  • 2
    Longer stride. A longer stride lets your body build more momentum toward the plate. Stretch it out a little more over time.
  • 3
    Loose, fast arm. A relaxed arm circles faster than a tense one. Speed lives in looseness, not muscle.
  • 4
    Hard wrist snap. The snap at release is free speed. Train it daily (see the wrist snap drill in the Drills tab).
  • 5
    Core and overall strength. Your core ties your legs and arm together. Strong athletes throw harder. Med ball work and planks help.
  • 6
    Glove-side pull. Snapping your glove arm into your body speeds up your rotation — like a figure skater pulling in their arms to spin faster.
🚫 Don't sacrifice mechanics chasing speed
📈 Speed comes in months, not days
💪 Get stronger, get faster
🎯

HITTING YOUR SPOTS — ACCURACY

🥅 Net or Catcher
⚡ Why

A pitcher who throws strikes and hits spots is worth more than a pitcher who throws hard and walks people. Location wins games at every level. This is a skill you build with focused reps.

  • 1
    Consistent mechanics first. You can't repeat a location if you can't repeat your motion. Accuracy is built on a repeatable delivery.
  • 2
    Pick a target every single pitch. Never just "throw it." Aim at a specific spot — a catcher's glove, a corner of a net, a taped square.
  • 3
    Divide the zone into 4 corners. Practice hitting low-inside, low-outside, high-inside, high-outside. Throw 5 to each spot.
  • 4
    Release point controls location. Out front = down in the zone. Early = high. Learn how your release changes where it goes.
  • 5
    Track your hits. Out of 20 pitches to a corner, how many hit the spot? Write it down. Watch the number climb over weeks.
🎯 Aim small, miss small
📊 Track your accuracy %
🔁 Same motion = repeatable spots
🎯 The Bullseye Drill

Hang an old bedsheet or pitching net and tape a target — or use a strike-zone net with corners. Throw 5 fastballs to each corner. The goal isn't speed here, it's repeating your spot. Do this 2–3 times a week and your control will jump fast.

THROW STRIKES. EVERYTHING ELSE FOLLOWS. 🎯

At 12, the pitcher who throws strikes plays. Speed and movement are great, but a pitcher who can locate a fastball on both corners is already ahead of most. Master this before you chase the fancy stuff.

Pitch #2 · Your Best Weapon

THE CHANGE-UP 🪂

If the fastball is your foundation, the change-up is your first real weapon. It's the most important second pitch in softball — and learning it now puts you years ahead.

Why The Change-Up Wins Games

Hitters time their swing to your fastball. The change-up looks exactly like your fastball — same windmill, same arm speed, same everything — but the ball comes out slower. The hitter swings way too early and misses, or makes weak contact. Deception is the entire point.

The secret that makes a change-up work: your arm must move at full fastball speed. The grip does the work of slowing the ball, not your arm. If you slow your arm down, the hitter sees it coming and it's useless. This is the hardest part to learn, which is why you start now.

🤚

BEGINNER GRIP: THE FLIP / BACKHAND CHANGE

⭐ Easiest To Learn
⚡ Why

There are several change-up grips, but the flip change (also called backhand change) is the easiest for beginners to control. Start here, then explore the circle change later as you advance.

  • 1
    Turn your hand so the back of your hand faces the catcher at release (palm faces you).
  • 2
    Grip the ball deeper in your hand — back toward your palm instead of your fingertips. This kills speed naturally.
  • 3
    At release, you "flip" the ball out the back of your hand instead of snapping it forward. No aggressive wrist snap.
  • 4
    Keep your arm speed full. Same circle, same drive, same everything. Only the grip and release change.
  • 5
    The ball comes out 8–12 mph slower than your fastball. That speed difference is what fools the hitter.
📈

THE PROGRESSION — BUILD IT STEP BY STEP

🙋 Mostly Solo
⚡ Why

You don't learn a change-up by walking out and throwing it full distance. You build it in stages. Master each stage before moving to the next. This is how you develop a change-up you can actually trust in a game.

  • 1
    Stage 1 — Grip & flip (no pitching). Just hold the grip and flip the ball into a net or to a partner from 10 feet. Get the release feel. Do this for days.
  • 2
    Stage 2 — Short distance. Add the full windmill motion but stand at half distance (about 20 ft). Focus on full arm speed with the slow grip.
  • 3
    Stage 3 — Full distance. Move back to the full pitching distance. Throw it for strikes. It'll feel weird at first — that's normal.
  • 4
    Stage 4 — Mix it in. In your bullpens, throw fastball, fastball, change-up. Practice making them look identical out of your hand.
  • 5
    Stage 5 — Locate it. A change-up works best low in the zone. Practice keeping it down. A high change-up gets hit hard.
⏳ Be patient — this takes weeks
🎭 Same arm speed always
⬇️ Keep it low in the zone
🧠

WHEN TO THROW IT

⭐ Pitch Strategy
⚡ Why

A change-up is only as good as your timing in throwing it. Throw it at the wrong time and you give away a free hit. Throw it at the right time and you get easy outs.

  • 1
    When the hitter is timing your fastball. If she's fouling off or catching up to your heat, slow her down with a change.
  • 2
    Ahead in the count (0-2, 1-2). You have a strike to spare. A change-up here gets ugly swings and strikeouts.
  • 3
    After back-to-back fastballs. Set it up. Two hard ones, then pull the string. The contrast is what makes it deadly.
  • 4
    Against aggressive, free-swinging hitters. Girls who swing hard at everything are the easiest to fool with a change.
  • 5
    NOT when you're behind 2-0 or 3-1 unless you fully trust it for a strike. Don't risk a walk on a pitch you're still learning.
FASTBALL SETS IT UP. CHANGE-UP FINISHES IT. 🪂

The fastball-change combo is the foundation of pitching at every level, from 12U all the way to college. Master these two pitches and you can get hitters out for years before you ever need a single movement pitch.

Down The Road · Future Pitches

MOVEMENT PITCHES 🌀

These are advanced pitches you'll grow into AFTER you own the fastball and change-up. We're introducing them now so you understand them — but don't rush here. Spin first, full pitches later.

📌 Read Before You Try These

  • ⏳ Movement pitches require a solid fastball foundation first — give it a full season.
  • 🌀 Start by learning SPIN, not full pitches. Spin drills are safe and build the feel.
  • 🩺 The rise ball especially can stress a young arm if rushed. Build slowly with a coach.
⬇️

THE DROP BALL

🔮 Learn First (of the two)
⚡ Why

The drop ball is usually the first movement pitch to learn because it's the most natural and the safest on your arm. The ball drops sharply as it reaches the plate, producing ground balls and swings over the top.

HOW IT WORKS
  • 1
    Topspin makes it drop. The ball spins forward (top rolls toward the plate). That forward spin pulls it down fast.
  • 2
    The "peel" drop (beginner version): Release slightly out front and "peel" your fingers up the back of the ball, like rolling up the back of it.
  • 3
    Get on top and out front. A longer stride and a release point further in front help create the downward angle.
  • 4
    Stay tall, finish down. Many coaches teach finishing the hand low as the ball rolls off the fingertips with topspin.
  • 5
    Great for ground balls. Thrown low, it produces weak grounders and swing-and-miss over the top. A workhorse pitch.
⬆️

THE RISE BALL

🔮 Advanced — Be Patient
⚡ Why

The rise ball is considered the hardest pitch in fastpitch softball. It appears to "rise" or stay up as it crosses the plate, producing pop-ups and swing-and-miss under the ball. This is a long-term project, not a this-month skill.

HOW IT WORKS
  • 1
    Backspin makes it rise. The ball spins backward (bottom rolls toward the plate). True backspin fights gravity and keeps the ball up.
  • 2
    You get UNDER the ball at release. Your hand works under and behind it, snapping upward to create that backspin.
  • 3
    It requires real arm speed. Without good velocity, a rise ball just hangs and gets crushed. That's why fastball speed comes first.
  • 4
    Thrown UP in the zone (or above it). The goal is to start it at the top of the zone so the hitter chases it up and out.
  • 5
    Learn it with a coach. The mechanics are tricky and easy to do wrong. This is the one pitch to learn with in-person instruction.
⏳ Truly a 1–3 year skill
🌀 Master spin before the full pitch
🩺 Build slowly to protect your arm
🌀 The Spin Drill — Start Here for Any Movement Pitch

Before throwing a single drop or rise, learn to create spin. Toss the ball straight up a few feet above your head and practice creating clean topspin (for drop) or backspin (for rise). Watch the rotation. This is a safe, solo drill that builds the exact feel you'll need later. Do it daily and you'll learn movement faster when it's time.

EARN THE FANCY PITCHES 🌀

Movement pitches are exciting, but they're the reward for mastering the basics. A 12-year-old who throws a locatable fastball and a deceptive change-up will get more outs than one who throws four mediocre pitches. Build your foundation first. The movement will come.

Daily Reps · Build The Skill

PITCHING DRILLS 💪

This is where the work happens. Drills marked 🙋 Solo you can do completely alone. Drills marked 🥅 Net/Catcher need a net or someone catching.

✋

WRIST SNAP DRILL

🙋 Solo · Daily
⚡ Why

The wrist snap is the single most important fundamental for a young pitcher. It creates both speed AND spin. This is the #1 drill you should do every day, and you can do it anywhere — even into a net in your bedroom.

🎯 Do: 3 sets × 20 snaps, daily
  • 1
    Stand a few feet from a net or wall. Hold the ball in your 4-seam grip.
  • 2
    Tuck your throwing elbow into your hip, forearm pointing forward. No arm circle — just the wrist.
  • 3
    Snap your wrist and release the ball into the net. The ball should come out with tight backspin and pop.
  • 4
    Watch the spin. Clean, tight backspin = good snap. Wobbly spin = keep working. Aim for the laces to spin in a clean line.
  • 5
    Progress to snapping without the net, catching it yourself, feeling the snap each time.
🛏️ Can do this in your room into a net
🌀 Watch for tight, clean spin
📅 Every single day
🌀

ARM CIRCLE DRILL (NO BALL)

🙋 Solo · Daily
⚡ Why

Before you can pitch fast, your arm circle has to be smooth, loose, and repeatable. Doing it with no ball lets you focus purely on the path and staying relaxed. Pure muscle memory work.

🎯 Do: 3 sets × 15 circles each arm
  • 1
    Stand in your pitching stance. No ball, empty hand.
  • 2
    Make slow, full arm circles, keeping your arm close to your ear at the top and relaxed throughout.
  • 3
    Brush your hip on the way past at the bottom. Feel where the release point is.
  • 4
    Gradually speed up the circle while staying loose. Loose = fast. Tense = slow.
  • 5
    Add your glove-side pull — snap the glove arm into your body as the throwing arm comes down.
🪞 Do it in front of a mirror
😌 Stay loose the whole circle
🧎

ONE-KNEE DRILL

🥅 Net/Catcher
⚡ Why

Taking your legs out of the equation forces you to focus on your arm circle, release point, and wrist snap. It isolates the upper-body mechanics so you can clean them up without thinking about your stride.

🎯 Do: 2 sets × 15 pitches
  • 1
    Kneel on your throwing-side knee, glove-side knee up, facing your target/net.
  • 2
    Do a full arm circle and release into the net, focusing on snap and release point.
  • 3
    Because you can't use your legs, all the power comes from your circle and snap. Great feedback.
  • 4
    Focus on releasing out in front and brushing your hip. Clean reps over hard reps.
👟

STRIDE & FREEZE DRILL

🙋 Solo · Daily
⚡ Why

Your stride and leg drive are where your power comes from. This drill builds a longer, more explosive, more balanced stride — and you can do it with no ball and no net, anywhere.

🎯 Do: 3 sets × 10 strides
  • 1
    Set up on your rubber (or a line). Explode forward into your stride.
  • 2
    Freeze when you land. Check your position: stride foot slightly open, good balance, weight transferring forward.
  • 3
    Measure your stride length. Try to add a little distance over time — a longer stride builds more power.
  • 4
    Hold the freeze for 2 seconds each rep to build balance and control in your landing.
📏 Track your stride distance
⚖️ Stick the landing balanced
🌀

SPIN DRILL

🙋 Solo · Daily
⚡ Why

Learning to control the spin you put on the ball is the foundation for every movement pitch you'll ever throw. It's also a great wrist-snap builder. Totally solo, totally safe, do it anywhere.

🎯 Do: 3 sets × 15 tosses
  • 1
    Toss the ball straight up a few feet above your head using only your wrist snap.
  • 2
    For backspin (rise feel): snap so the bottom of the ball rolls toward you. Laces spin in a clean line.
  • 3
    For topspin (drop feel): snap so the top of the ball rolls forward. Watch the rotation direction.
  • 4
    Catch it and check the spin. Clean, tight, consistent spin is the goal. This trains your hand for movement later.
👀 Watch the rotation closely
🎯 Tight clean spin = the goal
🎯

FULL-DISTANCE BULLPEN

🥅 Net/Catcher
⚡ Why

This is where it all comes together — full pitching from the regulation distance. A bullpen is a focused, structured pitching session, not just throwing. Quality and intent matter more than how many you throw.

🎯 Do: Structured session, 2–3x per week
  • 1
    Always warm up and arm-care first. Never start a bullpen cold.
  • 2
    Start with fastballs to spots. Hit each corner. Build a foundation before working anything else.
  • 3
    Throw with a purpose every pitch. Pick a target, commit, then check if you hit it. No lazy reps.
  • 4
    Work your change-up once your fastball feels good. Then movement spin if you're at that stage.
  • 5
    Finish on a good pitch. End the session feeling confident, never frustrated. Stop while it's going well.
  • 6
    Cool down and do recovery band work after. Protect the arm you just worked.
🎯 Every pitch has a target
📋 Quality over quantity
😊 Finish on a strike
Pitching IQ + Mental Game

STRATEGY & MINDSET 🧠

Pitching is as much mental as physical. The best pitchers think their way through an at-bat and stay calm under pressure. Here's how to compete with your brain, not just your arm.

🎯

PITCHING TO CONTACT vs CHASING STRIKEOUTS

Young pitchers think they need to strike everyone out. The truth: you have 8 defenders behind you. Letting hitters put the ball in play and trusting your defense is smart, low-stress pitching.

  • Throwing strikes and getting weak contact beats walking people chasing K's
  • Fewer pitches per batter means you last longer in the game
  • Your defense WANTS the ball — let them work
  • Strikeouts come naturally when you're ahead in counts
🔢

SIMPLE PITCH SEQUENCING

You don't need to overthink it as a beginner. A few simple patterns will make you tough to hit. As you grow, you'll add layers, but start here.

  • Get ahead early — throw a strike on the first pitch
  • Change speeds — a fastball then a change-up disrupts timing
  • Work both sides — in, then out keeps hitters off balance
  • When ahead 0-2 or 1-2, expand off the plate or pull the string
  • Never throw the same pitch in the same spot 3 times in a row
😤

THE MENTAL GAME — STAYING COMPOSED

You will walk batters. You will give up hits. Every pitcher does, even the best in the world. What separates good pitchers is how fast they reset and refocus.

  • One pitch at a time — forget the last one the instant it's over
  • Develop a reset routine: deep breath, step off, refocus on the catcher's glove
  • Bad call from the ump? Let it go. You can't control it. Next pitch.
  • Body language matters — stand tall and confident even when struggling
  • Your defense and teammates feed off your energy. Stay steady for them.
🔮

VISUALIZE & PREPARE

Top pitchers see success before it happens. Mental reps are real reps. Use this every time you pitch.

  • Before a game, picture yourself hitting your spots and competing calmly
  • Between pitches, see the pitch you want to throw before you throw it
  • Visualize handling pressure — bases loaded, full count, staying calm
  • Picture the result you want, then trust your mechanics to deliver it
  • Do 2–3 minutes of this the night before you pitch
🤝

WORKING WITH YOUR CATCHER

You have a built-in advantage here — you ARE a catcher. You understand the relationship from both sides. Use that.

  • Your catcher is your partner — trust her target and her calls
  • Because you catch, you know what's hard to handle — throw catchable strikes
  • Communicate between innings — what's working, what's not
  • A good pitcher-catcher pair runs the whole defense together
  • Knowing both positions will make you smarter than pitchers who only pitch
COMPOSURE IS A SUPERPOWER 😤

The pitcher who stays calm with the bases loaded is the one who gets out of the inning. You can't control hits, errors, or umpires. You CAN control your next pitch and your attitude. Master that and you'll out-compete more talented pitchers all the time.

Your Roadmap · Week By Week

WEEKLY PRACTICE PLAN 📅

Here's exactly what to do, week by week, as a brand-new pitcher. Follow this for your first month and you'll build a clean foundation the right way. Don't rush ahead — trust the build.

Week 1

FOCUS:
Motion & Wrist Snap
Day 1
Arm care + wrist snap drill (3×20) + arm circle drill no ball (3×15). Learn the feel. No full pitching yet.
Day 2
Arm care + wrist snaps + spin drill (3×15). Watch your spin get cleaner.
Rest
Light band work only. Let your body absorb the new movement.
Day 3
Arm care + stride & freeze drill (3×10) + arm circles. Start linking legs and arm slowly.
Day 4
Arm care + one-knee drill into net (2×15) + wrist snaps. First real releases into a net.
Rest
Full recovery. Stretch and hydrate.

Week 2

FOCUS:
Full Motion Together
Day 1
Arm care + put it together: half-speed full windmill pitches into a net (15–20). Focus on smooth, not hard.
Day 2
Arm care + wrist snaps + one-knee drill + 15 easy full pitches. Keep building the motion.
Rest
Band work + light stretching.
Day 3
Arm care + stride drill + 20 full pitches at 3/4 speed. Start aiming at a target.
Day 4
Arm care + first mini-bullpen: 25 fastballs to a target, full motion. Track how many are strikes.
Rest
Recovery day. Visualize good mechanics.

Week 3

FOCUS:
Strikes & Locations
Day 1
Arm care + bullpen: fastballs to all 4 corners (5 each). Add the bullseye drill. Track accuracy.
Day 2
Arm care + wrist snaps + spin drill + 20 location-focused pitches. Quality reps.
Rest
Band work. Core work — planks, med ball if available.
Day 3
Arm care + introduce the change-up: grip & flip drill (Stage 1) from short distance. Just the feel.
Day 4
Arm care + bullpen: 25 fastballs to spots + 10 change-up flips. Building the second pitch.
Rest
Recovery. You've earned it.

Week 4+

FOCUS:
Build The Combo
Day 1
Arm care + full bullpen: fastball locations + change-up progression Stage 2 (short distance, full arm speed).
Day 2
Arm care + velocity work: stride drill + leg drive focus + fast-arm full pitches. Plus daily wrist snaps.
Rest
Band work + strength: squats, planks, med ball throws.
Day 3
Arm care + bullpen mixing fastball + change-up at full distance. Make them look the same out of your hand.
Day 4
Arm care + spin drill for movement feel + full bullpen. Start thinking about pitch sequencing.

📌 A Note On This Schedule

This is a guide, not a rigid rule. Listen to your arm — if it's sore, take an extra rest day. Some weeks you'll repeat before moving on, and that's perfectly fine. Building a pitcher takes months and years, not weeks. The goal is steady, healthy, consistent progress. Quality reps beat rushed reps every time.

A LITTLE EVERY DAY BEATS A LOT ONCE A WEEK 📅

Fifteen good minutes of wrist snaps and arm circles every day will make you a better pitcher than one long session a week. Pitching rewards consistency more than almost any skill in sports. Show up daily and the results will come.
